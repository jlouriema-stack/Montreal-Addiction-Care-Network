Montreal Addiction Care Network

Method / How to read this project

This research-creation project translates an archival map into a public-history reading path. Each address is treated as a care node: a room, house, clinic, phone line, hospital, publication, or community space where addiction care became possible.

The project uses archival images, printed traces, institutional records, and mediated recovery narratives as a form of historical listening, asking how care moved before it became a stable system.

A.A. — Rooms Before Systems

A.A. turns crisis into rooms: jail cell, home, civic hall, and multilingual fellowship.

The birth of Montreal's first A.A. fellowship: The Drunk Tank at 2883 Saint-Charles Blvd., Kirkland

Dave B., or Dave Bancroft, anchors the first spark of A.A. in Montreal. His story belongs in the police cell: Easter weekend 1944, locked up for drunkenness, remembering the Alcoholics Anonymous book his sister Jean had given him, and reaching out to A.A. in New York from a place of crisis.

The response came through voice, mail, and identification. Bobbie told him, "I am an alcoholic. We have recovered." A copy of the Big Book arrived by mail the next day, followed by letters from Bobbie, Bill, and the New York office. By October 1944, New York was sending Dave inquiries from alcoholics and asking him to carry the message locally.

The Drunk Tank is the conversion point: jail cell, phone call, Big Book, correspondence, and one alcoholic becoming the contact through whom Montreal A.A. began to move.

The first A.A. meeting space in Montreal and Quebec: 4939 Av. Victoria

Dave B.'s home at 4939 Victoria Avenue became Quebec's first domestic recovery infrastructure. After the jail-cell crisis came a private house: basement meetings, crowded rooms, coffee, snacks, wives' labour, and the routine work of keeping a fellowship alive before any formal treatment system existed.

Victoria Avenue matters because it turns a single recovery story into a repeatable room. It is also where Dave later remembered the first two French Canadians learning about A.A., making the house an early bridge between anglophone fellowship and French-language recovery in Quebec.

If the Drunk Tank is crisis and contact, Victoria Avenue is domestic infrastructure: the home as meeting room, service hub, and first fragile recovery network.

Montreal Forum — A.A. Moves Into Public Civic Space: March 1945

After the first year of A.A. meetings in Dave B.'s home, Alcoholics Anonymous in Montreal moved into the Montreal Forum in March 1945.

The Forum gave recovery a public city address. A.A. moved from a private basement and living-room fellowship into one of Montreal's most recognizable civic buildings. It was not a hospital, treatment centre, or government program; it was a civic landmark temporarily turned into recovery infrastructure.

The sequence is the point: police cell → home → civic space. The Forum shows early recovery borrowing public rooms before Quebec had a formal addiction-treatment system.

Preston Hall — First French-Language A.A. Group 1947: 1173 Rue Drummond

Preston Hall moved A.A. across a linguistic threshold. By September 1947, French-speaking alcoholics had a meeting place of their own on Drummond Street, founded by Bob P., a friend of Dave B., after early attempts to bring French-speaking alcoholics into the fellowship.

The site turns recovery from an imported English-language fellowship into something Quebec could translate, inhabit, and expand. Language was not decoration here; it was infrastructure.

Kimberley House: 3652 Rue Sainte-Famille

Kimberley House turned one building into a multilingual recovery stack: English-speaking members on the main floor, French-speaking members upstairs, and the Hispano Group in the basement after November 1974.

According to A.A. Area 87, Alberto G. secured permission to use the largely empty basement, helping establish a meeting place that introduced A.A. to Montreal's growing Latin American and Hispanic community. Kimberley House shows A.A. adapting to Montreal's linguistic diversity through shared space, mutual aid, and community initiative.

Domrémy

Quebec's alcoholism-treatment backbone was stable, organized, and poorly built for youth drug crisis.

Domrémy

Domrémy was Quebec's major pre-1970 alcoholism-treatment apparatus: houses, reception rooms, referrals, detoxification, residential rehabilitation, supervision, and moral-pastoral care. Founded in 1957, Domrémy-Montréal emerged from Lacordaire and ecclesiastical networks, giving institutional form to an older Catholic anti-alcohol world rather than breaking from it.

  • 1957Domrémy-Montréal emerged from Lacordaire and ecclesiastical networks.
  • 1959Federation of Domrémy Houses founded: five centres and roughly forty reception rooms.
  • 1962Domrémy-Montréal treated 648 alcoholics in one year.
  • 1963-64Public-assistance status, daily per diem, and Ministry of Health grants followed.
  • 1970-71Toxicomania stayed marginal in the tables: two internal cases in 1970, thirty in 1971.

The Maison d'Entraide Domrémy began small and disciplined: seven beds, a ten-day cure, detoxification, rest, food, supervision, and moral support. A physician visited, but the chaplain sat near the centre of authority, recruiting patients, directing the house, and holding veto power in the early structure.

Expansion came fast. Founded in 1959, the Federation of Domrémy Houses linked five centres across Quebec and supervised roughly forty reception rooms. Domrémy-Montréal treated 648 alcoholics in 1962; by 1963-64 the houses had public-assistance status, a daily per diem, and special Ministry of Health grants.

Scale did not mean flexibility. By the turn of the 1970s, Domrémy-Québec reported 17,476 admissions since 1956, 10,464 internal treatments, and 4,399 persons served through external services since 1967; capacity had climbed from two beds at opening to eighty-two by the mid-1960s. Yet toxicomania remained marginal: two internal cases in 1970, thirty in 1971, and in 1972 a drug-treatment method the social-service team was still only beginning to develop.

Women-only Spaces

Women carved out gender-specific recovery rooms inside a male-dominated alcoholism culture.

Domrémy-Féminin: 2100 Rue de Londres

Domrémy-Féminin at 2100 rue de Londres marks formal, gendered alcoholism treatment inside the inherited Domrémy world. This address shows women entering institutional alcoholism care through a dedicated door.

2100 rue de Londres is the separate door into the official system. Even when the inherited system adapted for women, it remained built around alcoholism, institutional residence, and adult rehabilitation.

Women-Only A.A. Meeting, 1958: École St-Véronique

In June 1958, the A.A. Bulletin listed St. Véronique (Femmes) as a women-only Alcoholics Anonymous meeting at École St-Véronique, 4241 Parthenais. History often begins with men, police cells, basements, public halls, and founders, but women were also building the fellowship through courage, service, sponsorship, and their own safe meeting spaces.

St. Véronique brings women’s recovery into the story with real grace. The bulletin’s “Femmes” and “fermé” labels give us a rare trace of alcoholic women creating their own closed meeting space in 1950s Montreal, a room where they could speak freely, find one another, and build recovery away from shame and male-dominated public life.

There’s something beautiful here about “principles before personalities.” Before women were even fully welcome in many public drinking spaces in Montreal, A.A. already had women gathering in their own room for recovery, service, and fellowship.

Narcotics Anonymous

Early N.A. names the addict before Montreal has a durable drug-treatment system.

Lynn A. — N.A. Pioneer at Loyola Campus

Lynn A., publicly listed in a 1956 Montreal newspaper as Mlle Lynn Allen, was the central figure in the beginning of Narcotics Anonymous in Montreal. She came off narcotic drugs after a spiritual experience in a Windsor jail cell in 1951, found A.A. in 1953, and arrived in Montreal in November 1954.

In Montreal, Lynn attended A.A. as the only former narcotic addict she knew there, then found two others inside the fellowship. That small triangle became the basis for Montreal's first N.A. experiment. The first meeting was held on December 23, 1954, in a psychiatrist's apartment; Lynn had to work that night and made contact by phone.

By 1955, the experiment had a public-facing campus doorway: P.O. Box 361 in Westmount and weekly meetings at Loyola College. Lynn's later correspondence with Bill W. sharpened the deeper point: the problem was addiction beyond alcohol, and newcomers needed language that could name the addict directly.

Centre de l'Immaculée-Conception — Early Public N.A. Meeting, 1956

By 1956, Montreal Narcotics Anonymous had moved beyond Lynn A.'s fragile private-apartment beginning and into a public meeting space at the Centre de l'Immaculée-Conception, 4200 Bordeaux Street. A July 1956 newspaper article reported an open public meeting every Monday evening at 9 p.m.

Bordeaux gave early Montreal N.A. public legitimacy. Former narcotic addicts, physicians, municipal officials, clergy, and public-health figures appeared around the same experiment, arguing that drug addiction required more than the alcoholism model: hospitalization, treatment, rehabilitation, and mutual aid.

4200 Bordeaux was a brave public prototype. It proved that drug-specific mutual aid existed in Montreal long before the 1970 youth drug crisis, even if the structure around it remained fragile.

N.A. Central Office 1956

The St-Joseph office made early Montreal N.A. reachable.

The office turned N.A. from a meeting into a small administrative bridge between self-help and treatment. It could route people toward help, but it could not by itself guarantee money, beds, literature, staff, or long-term continuity.

Campion Book Shop — Addicts-Only N.A. Meeting 1956

Campion Book Shop gave early N.A. a protected addicts-only room. Bordeaux made the movement public; St-Joseph made it reachable; Campion protected identification itself.

Here, addicts could speak from direct experience without translating themselves for officials, relatives, alcoholics, or spectators.

The Rupture — Manseau

Manseau exposes the old system's limit: panic, hallucinogens, youth crowds, police, and medicine all at once.

Manseau

Manseau is the rupture marker: the moment Quebec's inherited alcoholism system had to confront a youth drug scene it was not built to govern. Domrémy could classify, supervise, and rehabilitate adult alcoholics; Manseau brought young people, LSD, outdoor crowds, panic, police, media spectacle, and improvised first-line care into one visible crisis.

Press reports described hundreds treated after drug use, hospital transfers, a mobile clinic with forty-three beds, major public costs, and hundreds of Sûreté du Québec officers. Guy Marcoux's account shows the care encounter from inside the trip: music, lights, bodies, crowds, marijuana, hashish, LSD, and physicians trying to work inside panic rather than above it.

Manseau exposed the limit of Quebec's inherited alcoholism system. Adult alcoholism institutions could classify, supervise, and rehabilitate; the festival demanded immediate youth-facing crisis care under conditions of panic, hallucinogens, crowds, police, medicine, and media spectacle. After Manseau, care had to move outward.

OPTAT

  • Jan. 1970OPTAT-linked Sainte-Anne external clinic activity is visible in the referral record.
  • Apr. 1970Lucien Laforest's OPTAT student-drug survey appeared.
  • Aug. 1970OPTAT's research service produced Le festival PDP Manseau.
  • Apr. 27, 1973OPTAT's scientific council met on toxicomanies.

OPTAT — the Office de prévention et du traitement de l'alcoolisme et des autres toxicomanies — belongs beside that rupture as Quebec's state-linked apparatus for prevention, research, treatment coordination, surveys, therapeutic-resource evaluation, and official expertise. In the sources used here, OPTAT is already active by early 1970: an OPTAT-linked Sainte-Anne external centre counted referrals between January 1970 and May 1971, with 749 consultations since January 1970.

OPTAT was already producing drug knowledge in 1970. Lucien Laforest's April 1970 survey mapped marijuana, LSD, hallucinogens, stimulants, tranquillizers, barbiturates, opiates, glue, alcohol, and tobacco across Montreal students; in August 1970, OPTAT's research service produced Le festival PDP Manseau.

For André Boudreau, OPTAT's leader and director of Manseau's medical centre, the festival was a clinical shock. He had publicly worried more about alcoholism than drugs, but after seeing young people in delirious LSD states, he rejected easy comparisons with alcohol or tobacco and treated youth drug use as a distinct problem demanding new knowledge.

By April 27, 1973, OPTAT's scientific council was meeting explicitly on toxicomanies. Its name shows the transition: alcohol still came first, while the "other toxicomanias" were being pulled into an official field moving faster than inherited institutions could absorb.

Drogue-Secours / Youth Clinics / Hospitals

After 1970, care moves outward into phones, print directories, clinics, hospitals, houses, and youth-trust services.

Drogue-Secours — Youth Crisis Care

Drogue-Secours was one of the clearest answers to Manseau. At 203 West Prince-Arthur, youth drug crisis became street-level emergency work: a twenty-four-hour phone line, a drop-in centre, calm talk during bad trips, hospital accompaniment, and trust built by young people for young people.

The space was deliberately non-institutional: coloured walls, little furniture, people sitting on the floor and talking. Later accounts show how wide the emergency network became: Drogue-Secours linked callers and visitors to hospital care, the Royal Victoria Hospital, the Youth Clinic on Sainte-Famille, volunteers, student nurses, drug-identification help, and CKGM radio warnings when dangerous drugs circulated.

By 1971-72, Drug Help was already describing itself as working in a vacuum, with too little money and too few social alternatives behind it; later, the old Drogue-Secours experiment gave way toward MUTATION after subsidies were cut. Its funding crisis showed how much of Montreal's youth drug care rested on emergency improvisation rather than stable public infrastructure.

203 Prince-Arthur became the live wire between the street, the clinic, the hospital, and the counterculture.

Kalatox — Rehabilitation After the Bad Trip 1972: 2533 rue Lafontaine, Montréal

Kalatox began where Drogue-Secours ended: after the bad trip, after the emergency referral, after the young person came down and still had to rebuild a life. Its name meant "Kaléidoscope d'aide aux toxicomanes," and the centre was created by young people, most of them former drug users themselves.

Quebec-Presse described Kalatox in March 1972 as a unique centre in Quebec: not mainly an emergency service, but a place of welcome, information, and rehabilitation for young drug users who wanted to restart their lives and find meaningful work. The model was practical and social: communication work, psychological exploration, group projects, workshops, prevention in schools and recreation centres, and referrals.

Kalatox treated abstinence as one part of a larger reconstruction of work, relationships, self-understanding, and social belonging. It also shows informal drug care beginning to brush up against official structures through federal Local Initiatives funding, OPTAT, probation officers, and the Court of Social Welfare.

Jewish General Hospital — E.D.M. Unit / Emergency Hospital Response 1970-1972

The Jewish General Hospital marks the emergency medical threshold in the youth drug network. By 1970, young people were arriving in crisis: overdoses, bad trips, panic states, dangerous mixtures, and psychiatric emergencies.

Le Dain Commission notes reported that seven beds had been opened at the Jewish General Hospital for drug users during the summer of 1970, while later countercultural service directories listed an E.D.M. Unit at 3755 Côte-Sainte-Catherine for emergency cases.

The Jewish General marks the point where the youth drug network crossed into hospital medicine. Peer workers could calm, translate, and accompany; emergency beds became necessary when the body or mind entered danger.

Westmount Youth Clinic: 4424 Sainte-Catherine Street West 1971-1974

The Westmount Youth Clinic, known simply as 4424, was confidential youth medicine after the 1970 drug rupture. At 4424 Sainte-Catherine Street West, young people could receive examination, counselling, referrals, and follow-up for drugs, alcohol, family conflict, sexuality, loneliness, and crisis.

Its importance was confidentiality. A teenager could come here with a problem they were too embarrassed, nervous, or afraid to bring to a family doctor, parent, police officer, or hospital. Under director Marjorie Bedoukian, 4424 treated the person before the label: not "delinquent," not "addict," not "problem child," but a young person with medical, emotional, social, and family realities tangled together.

By 1975, the clinic was seeing more use despite staff cuts and funding problems. 4424 reached young people who avoided parents, police, hospitals, and family doctors. Its closure by the mid-1970s shows the weakness of a care model built on grants, borrowed space, and exhausted staff.

LOGOS / Mainmise — underground print as referral infrastructure

Mainmise and LOGOS made care findable. In French and English, these countercultural publications translated the youth-drug crisis into practical instructions: addresses, service lists, crisis steps, drug warnings, specimen-analysis contacts, and concrete directions about where to go when things went wrong.

  • Jan. 1971Mainmise's Pénélope et le Québec alternatif circulated countercultural routes and services.
  • 1971LOGOS gave English-language street warnings and service-directory logic.
  • June 1972LOGOS/m.c.p. published Dope on Dope, pointing readers toward clinics and emergency units.
  • 1973Mainmise's Adresses utiles mapped a decentralized ecology of youth services.

Mainmise circulated routes through friends, experienced users, Drogue-Secours, specimen analysis, community services, and hospitals only when the situation exceeded what the milieu could handle. LOGOS performed the English-language version: street warnings, service directories, practical drug notes, and referrals to the Youth E.D.M. Unit at the Jewish General, the Westmount clinic, Milton Park, Head & Hands, the Black Community Centre, the St. Charles Community Clinic, and other low-threshold points of contact.

This was promotion in the punk-recovery sense: not polished advertising, but a survival map. At the same moment OPTAT was calling for drug education, underground print was already doing that work operationally, speaking in the language of use, risk, bad products, dosage uncertainty, trusted intermediaries, and where to turn before hospital capture.

By 1973, Mainmise's Adresses utiles and LOGOS service listings made the first line visibly cartographic. Youth care was being distributed through print, peer knowledge, clinics, crisis phones, storefront projects, and community networks before the state had fully reorganized the field.

Spera

Spera Montreal at 220 Mountain Street was the urban doorway into the wider Spera treatment world: a transition house between the street, the court, the hospital, the family, and the rural residential program. Spera began in Montreal in 1967 before its residential centre moved to Rawdon in May 1970.

Its name came from the Latin for hope, and its model was built around a hard break from the drug environment. In the Rawdon residence, therapy was collective: shared residence, work, meals, recreation, rules, responsibility, and constant conversation.

Gerry and Sharon Burton gave the project its lived-experience authority. Spera became a para-institutional bridge: residence without prison, discipline without Domrémy, and rehabilitation built around former drug users, labour, spirituality, and community life.

Spera turned post-1970 drug treatment into residence, routine, labour, and therapeutic community.

A.S.P.A. — Approche sécurisante des polytoxicomanes anonymes 1976

A.S.P.A. — Approche sécurisante des polytoxicomanes anonymes — marks one late-1970s endpoint of the post-Domrémy shift: specialized, drug-specific community treatment. Founded in 1976 by Jean Archambault, A.S.P.A. was created for people dependent on drugs of all kinds who could not find the information, support, or treatment they needed in organizations built mainly for alcoholics.

A.S.P.A. turned the post-1970 lesson into a specialized drug-treatment model: confidential, community-based, therapeutic, and organized around toxicomanie and polytoxicomanie rather than alcohol alone. It moved beyond A.A., Domrémy, and Catholic alcoholism treatment by answering a different population with a different vocabulary.

Its later philosophy makes the shift clearer: "anonymous" meant confidentiality rather than A.A.- or N.A.-style fellowship identity, and toxicomania was not framed simply as powerless disease. A.S.P.A. connected substance use to emotional, relational, social, and self-esteem problems while emphasizing responsibility, autonomy, and sometimes harm reduction rather than automatic abstinence.

A.S.P.A. shows the addiction system after Domrémy reorganizing around polydrug use, confidentiality, autonomy, emotional life, social context, and harm reduction.

Head & Hands

Head & Hands became an NDG free-clinic and youth-trust node after the 1970 drug rupture. Founded out of the YMCA youth-outreach world by Mark Earley and Pat Crawshaw around 1970-71, it grew from streetwork into a community service centre where teenagers and young adults could find medical help, legal advice, counselling, psychological referrals, sexual-health information, leisure programming, and drug education in one accessible place.

The response was trust before treatment. Workers met young people in parks, restaurants, clinics, and neighbourhood spaces, listened without moralizing, and gave information so they could make their own decisions. Its drug-and-alcohol education proposal named the lack of informed knowledge about long- and short-term effects, dependence, street-drug uncertainty, prescription misuse, and mixing drugs with alcohol.

Head & Hands placed drug education inside a wider youth survival network: health, law, sexuality, housing, family conflict, rights, and trust.

Conclusion

This map records the movement from backbone to network. Domrémy gave Quebec's pre-1970 alcoholism system its strongest institutional form: reception rooms, houses, detoxification, residential cure, moral authority, and public funding. That architecture stabilized adult alcoholism treatment, but it also fixed care inside a model that could not respond quickly to youth drug use, hallucinogens, bad trips, street crisis, and poly-drug realities.

Manseau made the fracture public. The festival forced police, doctors, youth, families, journalists, and emergency workers into the same field of crisis. Afterward, addiction care in Montreal increasingly moved through the spaces Domrémy could not supply: crisis phones, peer drop-ins, youth clinics, hospital emergency units, underground print directories, transition houses, and community-based poly-drug treatment.

By 1977, Montreal addiction care had changed location, language, and method. It no longer belonged to a single institution or a single diagnosis. It had become a dispersed ecology of rooms, houses, clinics, phone lines, publications, hospitals, and neighbourhood services. The formal system did not lead every part of that transformation. Often, it followed the people who had already begun building care elsewhere.
