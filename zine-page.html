A.A. — Rooms Before Systems

5 research sites folded into one section of the Montreal care story.

The birth of Montreal's first AA fellowship: The Drunk Tank at 2883 Saint-Charles Blvd. Kirkland, Quebec

Dave B., or Dave Bancroft, was one of the key founders of Alcoholics Anonymous in Canada and the central figure in the beginning of A.A. in Montreal and Quebec. His story appears in the A.A. Big Book, the main foundational text of Alcoholics Anonymous, which contains the program’s basic ideas, the Twelve Steps, and personal recovery stories from alcoholics who found sobriety. In his Big Book story, “Gratitude in Action,” Dave describes taking his first drink as a teenager in Sherbrooke and feeling immediately transformed by alcohol. Over the years, drinking cost him work, health, stability, and peace of mind; he suffered serious injuries, hospitalization, paralysis, psychiatric stays, fear, loneliness, and deep depression.

On Easter weekend in 1944, he found himself in a Montreal jail cell, at SPVM Poste 1, after being locked up for drunkenness. In that crisis, he remembered a book his sister Jean had given him about Alcoholics Anonymous and tried to reach A.A. in New York. He eventually spoke with Bobbie, who told him, “I am an alcoholic. We have recovered.” Dave realized people hundreds of miles away cared enough to help him. A copy of the Big Book arrived by mail the next day, followed by letters from Bobbie, Bill, and others in the New York office. By October 1944, they were sending Dave inquiries from alcoholics and asking him to help carry the Fellowship where he was. For the first year, meetings were held in his home, and Dave later remembered that the first French Canadians to learn about A.A. did so in his basement.

In conclusion, Montreal recovery begins here before treatment centres, hospitals, or state addiction policy: in a jail cell, through a book, a phone call, spiritual awakening, correspondence, and one alcoholic carrying the message to another.

The first A.A. meeting space in Montreal and Quebec: 4939 Av. Victoria

Dave B.’s home at 4939 Victoria Avenue became the first real recovery room in Quebec after his Easter 1944 spiritual awakening. In his Big Book story, “Gratitude in Action,” Dave remembered that for the first year, all A.A. meetings in Quebec were held in his house. People filled the home while members gathered for closed meetings, and the wives of members waited in the bedroom or kitchen, making coffee and snacks while wondering what would happen to the men. This home also mattered linguistically: Dave later wrote that the first two French Canadians to learn about A.A. did so in the basement of his house, making 4939 Victoria one of the birthplaces of French-language A.A. in Quebec.

This site matters because it shows the first major movement in Montreal recovery geography: from the police cell to the private home, from crisis to fellowship, from one alcoholic’s awakening to a living-room network that began carrying the message across the city.

Montreal Forum — A.A. Moves Into Public Civic Space: March 1949

After the first year of A.A. meetings in Dave B.’s home, Alcoholics Anonymous in Montreal moved into the Montreal Forum in March 1945. This was a major shift in the geography of recovery: A.A. went from a private basement and living-room fellowship into one of the city’s most recognizable public buildings. The Forum was not a hospital, treatment centre, or government program; it was a civic landmark temporarily turned into recovery infrastructure. This elucidates how early addiction care in Montreal grew by borrowing ordinary and public rooms before the province had a formal addiction-treatment system. In sum, recovery moved from the police cell, to Dave B.’s home, and then into public civic space.

Preston Hall — First French-Language A.A. Group 1947: 1173 Rue Drummond

Preston Hall on Drummond Street became the meeting place for the first French-language Alcoholics Anonymous group in Montreal in September 1947. The group was founded by Bob P., a friend of Dave B., after early attempts to bring French-speaking alcoholics into the fellowship. This site marks a major linguistic shift in Montreal recovery history. A.A. had already moved from the police cell to Dave B.’s home and then to the Montreal Forum; at Preston Hall, recovery crossed more fully into French-language Quebec life. In sum, addiction care in Montreal had to be translated socially, culturally, and linguistically before it could become a wider Quebec movement.

Kimberly House: 3652 Rue Sainte-Famille

Kimberley House (3652 rue Sainte-Famille) became one of Montreal's first multilingual A.A. spaces, where English-, French-, and Spanish-speaking recovery communities shared a single building. On November 4, 1974, Montreal's first Spanish-speaking A.A. group—the Hispano Group—began meeting in the basement, while English-speaking members met on the main floor and French-speaking members upstairs. According to A.A. Area 87, Alberto G. secured permission to use the largely empty basement, helping establish a meeting place that introduced A.A. to Montreal's growing Latin American and Hispanic community. Kimberley House shows how recovery was built through shared spaces, language, and mutual aid before the group later moved to 4780 rue Coloniale.

Domrémy

1 research sites folded into one section of the Montreal care story.

Domrémy

Domrémy was Quebec’s major pre-1970 alcoholism-treatment apparatus: a province-wide network of houses, reception rooms, referrals, detoxification, residential rehabilitation, supervision, and moral-pastoral care. It grew out of Catholic temperance, Lacordaire, and ecclesiastical networks rather than from a secular drug-treatment field. Domrémy-Montréal emerged in 1957 from that older anti-alcohol world, and the early Maison d’Entraide Domrémy offered a small residential cure built around detoxification, rest, food, supervision, moral support, and medical visitation. A physician visited the house, but the chaplain occupied central authority in the early structure, recruiting patients, directing the house, and holding veto power.

Its larger system was built around adult alcoholism: intake, screening, short detoxification, longer readaptation, return to sobriety networks, and follow-up through reception rooms. Those rooms ideally joined a social worker, a physician, and a chaplain, while A.A. and Lacordaire members appeared in the therapeutic world through testimony, sobriety support, and moral reinforcement. Domrémy therefore fused four forces into one institution: Catholic moral authority, Lacordaire associational discipline, medical supervision, and social-service rehabilitation. By the 1960s, Domrémy had become the dominant institutional centre of Quebec alcoholism care. The Federation of Domrémy Houses, founded in 1959, linked five centres and supervised roughly forty reception rooms.

Domrémy-Montréal treated 648 alcoholics in 1962 alone, receiving referrals from Lacordaire circles, Alcoholics Anonymous, courts, and social-service agencies. Domrémy houses occupied a special hospital category under Quebec public assistance and received per diem and special grants from the Ministry of Health by 1963–64. By the early 1970s, Domrémy-Québec reported 17,476 admissions since 1956, 10,464 internal treatments, and 4,399 people served through external services since 1967; its capacity had risen to eighty-two beds by the mid-1960s, and expenditures rose from about $98,000 in 1961 to more than $744,000 in 1970. Compared with other therapeutic resources in 1972, Domrémy-Québec had eighty-two beds, St-Michel had twenty-five, OPTAT Ste-Foy had none, and Boule-de-Neige had twelve places in a small house-based model.

In inpatient alcoholism care, Domrémy held a de facto monopoly. Domrémy’s key people and constituencies were layered rather than centred on one founder. At the base were Catholic clergy, chaplains, moral counsellors, physicians, social workers, Lacordaire members, A.A. witnesses, reception-room volunteers, and provincial health administrators. The Lacordaire connection was structural: roughly half of those responsible for the clinic came from Lacordaire circles, Domrémy-Montréal’s charter required eight of fifteen administrators to come from Lacordaire services, and two-thirds of the corporation board also belonged to the circles. Women-Only A.A. Meeting 1958: École St-Véronique —

In June 1958, the A.A. Bulletin listed St. Véronique (Femmes) as a women-only Alcoholics Anonymous meeting at École St-Véronique, 4241 Parthenais. history often begins with men, police cells, basements, public halls, and founders, but women were also building the fellowship through courage, service, sponsorship, and their own safe meeting spaces. In the wider history of A.A., women such as Marty M. helped prove that alcoholic women could recover, lead, and carry the message to others. St. Véronique belongs in that lineage. It was a room where women could speak honestly, identify with one another, and find recovery among other women at a time when alcoholism carried heavy shame, especially for women.

The bulletin clipping marks the meeting as “Femmes” and “fermé,” meaning it was not open to men, making it a small but powerful trace of women’s recovery organizing in 1950s Montreal. In sum, St. Véronique shows that recovery was not only built by institutions or famous founders; it was also built by women gathering in ordinary rooms and keeping the door open for the next woman who needed help.

Narcotics Anonymous

4 research sites folded into one section of the Montreal care story.

Lynn A. — N.A. Pioneer at Loyola Campus

Lynn A., publicly listed in a 1956 Montreal newspaper as Mlle Lynn Allen, was the central figure in the beginning of Narcotics Anonymous in Montreal. She was an ex-addict who had found her way into Alcoholics Anonymous, but she understood that drug addicts needed language, meetings, and literature that spoke directly to them. In 1951, she came off narcotic drugs after a spiritual experience in a county jail cell in Windsor, Ontario; she later found A.A. in 1953. By 1954, Lynn was in Montreal, attending A.A. meetings as the only ex-addict she knew there. After hearing about Danny C. and Narcotics Anonymous in New York, she contacted him and received literature. When she moved to Montreal in November 1954, she met two other ex-addicts in A.A.; one of them, a psychiatrist, suggested that they had enough people to start an N.A. group.

The first Montreal N.A. meeting was held on December 23, 1954, in that psychiatrist’s apartment, with two people attending, while Lynn made contact by phone because she had to work that night. By 1955, the fragile apartment experiment had gained a public-facing campus doorway: people seeking N.A. could write to P.O. Box 361 in Westmount or attend weekly meetings at Loyola College on Sherbrooke Street West, Wednesday evenings at 8 o’clock. Loyola matters because it gives early Montreal N.A. an addressable institutional room. A Catholic college campus became one of the first visible places where drug-specific recovery could gather in the city. Lynn’s later correspondence with Bill W., co-founder of A.A., shows the deeper meaning of her work.

She argued that alcoholism was addiction to alcohol and that addiction itself was the real problem, not the particular chemical. She adapted A.A.’s First Step so that newcomers could say, “We admitted we were addicts,” because she knew it was almost impossible to tell someone who had just put down a hypodermic needle that their real problem was simply booze. In sum, Lynn A. and the Loyola campus site mark the transition from alcoholic to addict, from borrowed A.A. language to drug-specific mutual aid, and from private apartment meetings to a Montreal fellowship beginning to name itself in public.

The Second and last Official N.A Meeting Before it's collapse until the Aids Crisis: 4200 Bordeaux Street, The Centre de l’Immaculée-Conception 1956

By 1956, Montreal Narcotics Anonymous had moved beyond Lynn A.’s fragile private-apartment beginning and into a public meeting space at 4200 Bordeaux Street, connected to the Centre de l’Immaculée-Conception. A July 1956 newspaper article reported that N.A. held an open public meeting there every Monday evening at 9 p.m. The same article shows how ambitious the Montreal experiment had become: it described a movement of former narcotic addicts helping one another, modeled partly on Alcoholics Anonymous, but also argued that drug addiction required something beyond the alcoholism model, including access to hospitalization, treatment, and rehabilitation. This was not only a peer meeting; it was an attempt to build a hybrid structure around addicts, physicians, municipal officials, clergy, and public-health authority.

Lynn Allen was listed as secretary, while figures such as Dr. Adélard Groulx, Dr. R. L. Stanford, W. H. Outerbridge, P. R. Judge, and Father James Dwyer appeared around the organization. The Bordeaux site matters because it shows early Montreal N.A. trying to become public, organized, and socially legitimate before the city had any stable drug-treatment system. But it also shows why the experiment struggled. A meeting room could not carry the whole weight of drug treatment. The group needed money, members, literature, hospital access, professional backing, and follow-up care. The 2013 N.A. Chronology later makes that fragility clear: Canadian N.A. had small beginnings but insufficient numbers to provide the full support addicts needed, and Lynn’s 1957 correspondence with Bill W.

shows the group depending on a short-term $3,000 Quebec grant to carry it for a year or until new literature could be written. In sum, 4200 Bordeaux was a brave prototype, not a finished system. It proved that drug-specific mutual aid existed in Montreal long before the 1970 youth drug crisis, but it also exposed the gap between naming the addict and building a durable care structure.

N.A. Central Office 1956

The central office at 1014 East St-Joseph Boulevard marks the moment early Montreal N.A. tried to become visible, reachable, and administratively real. Unlike the Bordeaux meeting room, this site was not mainly about gathering in fellowship; it was about building a public contact point for addicts, families, hospitals, doctors, clergy, welfare workers, and civic officials. The July 1956 Dimanche-Matin article presented the office as the headquarters of the Montreal branch of the Narcotics Anonymous International Foundation, gave a telephone number, and directed readers to contact the group for information. That matters because early N.A. was trying to do more than hold meetings.

It was trying to create a bridge between self-help and treatment: a place where someone could call, ask for help, be referred, and possibly be connected to hospitalization or rehabilitation. The office also shows the movement’s weakness. A phone number and a civic committee could make N.A. public, but they could not guarantee money, beds, literature, staff, or long-term continuity. In sum, 1014 East St-Joseph was the administrative face of a fragile drug-recovery experiment: bold enough to announce itself to the city, but still too underbuilt to become a durable treatment system.

Campion Book Shop — Addicts-Only N.A. Meeting 1956

Campion Book Shop at 1249 Metcalfe Street marks a different kind of early N.A. space: not a public meeting, not an office, but an addicts-only room. This site matters because it shows early Montreal N.A. drawing a boundary around who needed to be in the room. The public meeting at Bordeaux made N.A. visible, and the St-Joseph office made it reachable, but an addicts-only meeting created a protected space where people could speak from direct experience without translating themselves for outsiders, officials, family members, or alcoholics. That boundary was historically important. Lynn A. and other early N.A. figures were trying to adapt the A.A. recovery form to a different identity: the addict.

Campion Book Shop therefore marks the moment when the fellowship was not only borrowing rooms, phone lines, and institutional support, but also protecting identification itself. In sum, this site shows early N.A. naming the addict as someone who needed their own language, their own meeting, and their own room.

The Rupture — Manseau

1 research sites folded into one section of the Montreal care story.

Manseau and Optat

Manseau should be mapped as the rupture marker in this project: the moment when Quebec’s inherited alcoholism system was forced to confront a youth drug scene it had not been built to govern. Domrémy could classify, treat, supervise, and rehabilitate adult alcoholics, but Manseau exposed a different problem altogether: young people, LSD, outdoor gatherings, panic, media spectacle, police presence, emergency medicine, and improvised first-line care. In the summer of 1970, press coverage turned the festival into a public crisis. Le Nouvelliste described drugs as the central force at the event, reported hundreds of young people treated after drug use, and linked most cases to LSD, with some hashish cases and hospital transfers.

Other reports described thousands moving through the site, hundreds of Sûreté du Québec officers, a mobile clinic with forty-three beds, and major public costs. The numbers matter because they show scale; the meaning matters more. Manseau compressed scattered anxieties about schools, families, youth culture, pop festivals, drugs, policing, medicine, and moral authority into one visible scene. Guy Marcoux’s account shows what that crisis looked like from inside the care encounter. He described Manseau as a mobile youth world shaped by music, lights, bodies, crowds, marijuana, hashish, LSD, and other substances, where the physician had to work inside the trip rather than above it. Care meant reassurance, observation, calm speech, and sometimes tranquilizers or restraint when hallucinations became terrifying and would not break.

Marcoux later framed Manseau as a kind of “regulated drunkenness,” with little alcohol and many hallucinogens, and as a moment when frightened young people had to be brought back to solid ground. Drogue-Secours turns that lesson into the clearest first-line model. In La Patrie, Jean Leblanc described helping doctors at Manseau treat young people in “break-out”: for hours, they spoke to adolescents to bring them down and reassure them. That detail is the heart of the rupture. The work was not long-term rehabilitation, confession, discipline, or institutional readaptation. It was presence: staying with frightened young people, calming them, helping them survive the trip, and keeping panic from becoming catastrophe. Drogue-Secours later built that principle into an urban emergency service run by young people for young people.

Its method was direct: if someone was on a bad trip, alone, afraid of their parents, or trapped in nightmares, they could call, come in, or be picked up. The staff would talk calmly, accompany them to medical help if needed, and stay with them until the crisis passed. In sum, Manseau belongs on the map because it shows the old alcoholism backbone reaching its limit. After Manseau, care had to move outward into youth clinics, hospital emergency beds, bad-trip lines, experimental houses, street-level information, and community-based drug services. Manseau was the rupture; Drogue-Secours was one of the answers.

Drogue-Secours — Youth Crisis Care

Drogue-Secours at 203 West Prince-Arthur Street marks the rupture point in this map: the youth drug crisis forced care out of formal alcoholism institutions and into street-level, peer-run emergency work. In 1970, La Patrie described Drogue-Secours as a drop-in, information, and reception centre run by young people for young people. Its basic service was presence. When someone was caught in a bad trip or “freak-out,” afraid, panicking, having nightmares, or scared of their parents’ reaction, Drogue-Secours offered someone who could talk calmly, help them come down, accompany them to medical care if needed, and stay with them until the crisis passed.

The centre was open twenty-four hours a day, with a continuous telephone service, and its space was deliberately non-institutional: coloured walls, little furniture, people sitting on the floor and talking. This was not Domrémy, not A.A., and not a hospital ward. It was a new form of crisis care built from youth language, trust, lived experience, and immediate action. Later accounts show how wide that emergency network became: Drogue-Secours linked callers and visitors to hospital care, the Royal Victoria Hospital, the Youth Clinic on Sainte-Famille, volunteers, student nurses, drug-identification help, and even CKGM radio warnings when dangerous drugs were circulating. Its fragility matters as much as its courage.

By 1971–72, Drug Help was already describing itself as working in a vacuum, with too little money and too few social alternatives behind it; later, the old Drogue-Secours experiment gave way toward MUTATION after subsidies were cut. That ending proves the larger point: Drogue-Secours did not appear because the system worked. It appeared because the existing system could not speak to youth drug use fast enough. In sum, 203 Prince-Arthur was a living emergency line between the street, the clinic, the hospital, and the counterculture.

Kalatox — Rehabilitation After the Bad Trip 1972: 2533 rue Lafontaine, Montréal

Kalatox at 2533 rue Lafontaine marks the next stage after Drogue-Secours. Where Drogue-Secours answered the immediate crisis — the bad trip, the panic call, the emergency referral — Kalatox tried to answer the harder question: what happens after the young person comes down and still has to live? Its name meant “Kaléidoscope d’aide aux toxicomanes,” and the centre was created by young people, most of them former drug users themselves. Quebec-Presse described it in March 1972 as a unique centre in Quebec: not mainly an emergency service, but a place of welcome, information, and rehabilitation for young drug users who wanted to restart their lives and find work that meant something to them. The model was practical and social.

Kalatox offered personal welcome, communication work, psychological exploration, group projects, workshops, preventive information in schools and recreation centres, and referral to outside services when needed. Its larger dream was a three-stage path: a city drop-in centre, a rehabilitation farm, and then reintegration through workshops or new work projects. Le Devoir added the crucial point: drug use was often the result of school, family, and socio-economic problems, so simply quitting drugs was not enough. Kalatox tried to rebuild contact with life, work, other people, and society itself. This site matters because it shows the post-1970 system moving past emergency rescue into youth-led rehabilitation.

It also shows how informal drug care began to brush up against official structures: Kalatox received federal Local Initiatives funding, worked closely with OPTAT, and attracted interest from probation officers and the Court of Social Welfare. In sum, Kalatox was not just a place to stop using. It was a place where young people tried to build a second life after drugs, outside the old alcoholism model and closer to the realities of youth, work, alienation, and community.

Jewish General Hospital — E.D.M. Unit / Emergency Hospital Response 1970-1972

The Jewish General Hospital site marks the hospital side of the youth drug rupture. By 1970, Montreal’s drug crisis could no longer be handled only through alcoholism treatment, police response, A.A.-style mutual aid, or street-level peer support. Young people were arriving in crisis: overdoses, bad trips, panic states, dangerous mixtures, and psychiatric emergencies. The Jewish General became one of the places where that crisis entered hospital medicine. Le Dain Commission notes reported that seven beds had been opened at the Jewish General Hospital for drug users during the summer of 1970, while later countercultural service directories listed an “E.D.M. Unit” at the Jewish General, 3755 Côte-Sainte-Catherine, for emergency cases. This site matters because it shows that the new drug-care network was tiered.

Drogue-Secours and similar groups could calm, accompany, refer, and translate youth experience; hospitals were needed when the body or mind crossed into danger. The E.D.M. Unit therefore represents emergency capacity rather than long-term rehabilitation. It was one node in a wider survival chain: friend, peer helper, drug-help line, clinic, ambulance, hospital bed. In sum, the Jewish General Hospital shows the rupture becoming medical without becoming fully institutionalized. Drug care had moved outward into the street and community, but the emergency door still led back into the hospital.

Westmount Youth Clinic: 4424 Sainte-Catherine Street West 1971-1974

The Westmount Youth Clinic, known simply as 4424, was one of the clearest examples of youth medicine after the 1970 drug rupture. Located at 4424 Sainte-Catherine Street West, it offered medical clinics, individual and group counselling, psychological testing, nutritional advice, drop-in space, emergency calls, and referrals for young people dealing with drugs, alcohol, family conflict, sexuality, loneliness, and crisis. Its importance was confidentiality. A teenager could come here with a problem they were too embarrassed, nervous, or afraid to bring to a family doctor, parent, police officer, or hospital. Under director Marjorie Bedoukian, 4424 treated the person before the label: not “delinquent,” not “addict,” not “problem child,” but a young person with medical, emotional, social, and family realities tangled together.

By 1975, the clinic was seeing more use despite staff cuts and funding problems: hundreds came for medical care and counselling in only six months, and staff described the centre as a “last hope” for many. Hal Gray, one of its key youth workers, remembered the drug scene changing in real time: hallucinogens, heroin, and mescaline gave way to pharmaceutical drugs, then alcohol became just as serious a problem. This site matters because 4424 shows the system moving from emergency bad-trip response into sustained youth-facing care. It was not a hospital ward, not Domrémy, and not a police program. It was a local clinic where young people could talk, be examined, be counselled, be referred, and be followed up.

Its ending also matters: by 1975 the building was sold, the clinic was forced out, and by 1977 local reports were still describing the need for youth drop-in facilities after 4424 had closed. In sum, Westmount Youth Clinic shows both the power and fragility of community-based youth care: it reached the young people the old system missed, but it survived hand-to-mouth, dependent on grants, donations, borrowed space, and exhausted staff.

Spera

Spera Montreal at 220 Mountain Street marks the urban doorway into the wider Spera treatment world: a transition house between the street, the court, the hospital, the family, and the rural residential program. Spera had begun in Montreal in 1967 before its residential centre moved to Rawdon in May 1970. Its name came from the Latin for hope, and its model was built around a hard break from the drug environment. The idea was simple and forceful: the young drug user had to be removed from the immediate scene of use and placed in a house where recovery could become daily life. In the Rawdon residence, that meant voluntary admission, group therapy, work, school, house maintenance, cooking, sports, rules, responsibility, and constant contact with others trying to get clean.

Gerry and Sharon Burton gave the project its lived-experience authority. Gerry was described as a former drug user who could speak to young people because he had been there himself; his philosophy was that if he could do it, they could do it too. By the early 1970s, Spera was described less like a clinic and more like a therapeutic community: people played together, worked together, ate together, lived together, and above all talked together. This site matters because Spera shows the post-rupture system becoming more structured than Drogue-Secours or Kalatox, but still outside the old alcoholism institution. It was not Domrémy, not a hospital ward, and not just a hotline.

It was a para-institutional bridge: discipline without prison, care without a formal state system, and rehabilitation built around ex-users, residence, spirituality, labour, and community life. In sum, Spera Montreal shows how drug treatment after 1970 moved into halfway houses, transition homes, referral networks, and therapeutic communities when the older alcoholism system could not carry the new drug crisis.

A.S.P.A. — Approche sécurisante des polytoxicomanes anonymes 1976

A.S.P.A. — Approche sécurisante des polytoxicomanes anonymes — marks the last stage of this map: drug treatment becoming its own specialized community structure after the failures and limits of alcoholism-based care. Founded in 1976 by Jean Archambault, A.S.P.A. was created for people dependent on drugs of all kinds who could not find the information, support, or treatment they needed in movements and organizations built mainly for alcoholics. That origin makes this site perfect for the thesis. A.S.P.A. did not simply extend A.A., Domrémy, or Catholic alcoholism treatment; it answered a different population with a different vocabulary: toxicomanie, polytoxicomanie, non-medical detox, rehabilitation, confidentiality, responsibility, autonomy, and life in the person’s own environment. This is what came after the crack in the system.

Drogue-Secours handled panic and bad trips. Kalatox imagined rehabilitation and work. Spera built residential community away from the drug scene. A.S.P.A. turned the post-1970 lesson into a drug-specific support model: structured, therapeutic, anonymous, but not simply A.A. or N.A. under another name. Its later philosophy makes the rupture even clearer: A.S.P.A. explicitly describes “anonymous” as confidentiality rather than a reference to A.A.- or N.A.-style fellowships, and it does not frame toxicomania simply as a powerless disease. Instead, it treats substance use as a symptom connected to emotional, relational, social, and self-esteem problems, while emphasizing responsibility, autonomy, and sometimes harm reduction rather than automatic abstinence. In sum, A.S.P.A.

shows the addiction system after Domrémy: no longer organized only around alcohol, no longer only emergency street care, and no longer dependent on borrowed recovery rooms, but moving toward specialized drug treatment for polytoxicomania.

Head & Hands

Head & Hands belongs on this map as an NDG free-clinic and youth-trust node created after the 1970 drug rupture, when young people needed help that did not begin with judgment, police, hospitals, or institutional labels. Founded out of the YMCA youth-outreach world by Mark Earley and Pat Crawshaw around 1970–71, Head & Hands grew from streetwork into a community service centre where teenagers and young adults could find medical help, legal advice, counselling, psychological referrals, sexual-health information, leisure programming, and drug education in one accessible place. Its own early history described 1969–70 as an “Age of Experimentation,” marked by drug abuse, overdoses, freak-outs, unwanted pregnancies, sexually transmitted diseases, the generation gap, and youth distrust of adult bureaucratic institutions.

The response was trust before treatment: workers met young people in parks, restaurants, clinics, and neighbourhood spaces, listened without moralizing, and gave information so they could make their own decisions. Its drug-and-alcohol education proposal sharpened that approach by naming the lack of informed knowledge among young people about long- and short-term effects, dependence, street-drug uncertainty, prescription misuse, and mixing drugs with alcohol; the project proposed student health workers, outreach sessions, a resource library, and modules on alcohol, depressants, stimulants, hallucinogens, narcotics, and over-the-counter drugs.

Head & Hands therefore shows youth care after Manseau becoming broader than bad-trip rescue: health, law, sexuality, drugs, housing, rights, family conflict, and survival all entered the same first-line community doorway.
